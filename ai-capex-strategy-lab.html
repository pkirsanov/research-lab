6 · The research behind every number

Thesis & the scoring framework

The binding constraint of the AI buildout has walked downstream: silicon → memory → advanced packaging → power → the grid → fuel → cooling → water → skilled labor. The market has repriced the first few links hard. Edge lives further down, where inputs are capacity-inelastic, geopolitically concentrated, sometimes a tiny % of the bill-of-materials yet on the critical path — which is exactly what gives them pricing power.

Net-edge model

Net edge ≈ Scarcity × Spiking-now × (1 / supply-response speed) × (1 − crowding) × investability, timed by trigger.

  • Structural chokepoint (HOLD): spiking and supply can't scale for years → durable pricing power.
  • Cyclical spike (TRADE): spiking but capacity floods in ~18–24 months → mean-reverts. The supply rush that creates the spike also ends it.

This tool encodes each name's scarcity tier, trigger timing, bull return, downside, volatility and thesis-probability as editable assumptions, then aggregates them into return / probability / risk for each horizon.

Corrections log (what earlier drafts got wrong)
  • Memory/storage was missing. The most concrete live "AI rush → price spike" is HBM crowding out commodity DRAM/NAND, dragging DDR5/DDR4, enterprise SSD and nearline HDD into shortage. Now central.
  • Cooling overstated. Near-term AI racks use water/glycol direct-to-chip, not exotic dielectric fluids. The real choke is cold plates, quick-disconnects, manifolds, CDUs.
  • Cleveland-Cliffs was a bad GOES "pure play" — electrical steel is immaterial to its auto-steel earnings. Replaced by Powell / Eaton / nVent.
  • Rare earths are adjacent, not a direct datacenter input (motors, grid, robotics, defense). Treated as a strategic-premium parallel trade.
  • Gas turbines are long-lead (2027–30 CODs). The near-term on-site bridge is reciprocating gas engines / gensets / aeroderivatives + fuel cells.
  • Gallium/germanium already partly spiked after China's Dec-2024 export ban — some gain realized; remaining thesis is Western supply build.
  • AXT cuts both ways — a GaAs/InP/Ge substrate maker that is China-based and hurt by China's own licensing.
  • June-30 refresh: added AI networking/custom ASICs (AVGO, MRVL, ANET, CIEN, GLW, NOK), server/integrator suppliers (DELL, HPE, SMCI), packaging services (AMKR, ASX), EDA/design IP (SNPS, CDNS), buildout/cooling/EPC/REITs (EME, FIX, TT, DOV, PH, DLR, EQIX), and contracted power (TLN, NRG). Removed cloud-capacity buyers such as ORCL/CRWV/NBIS/IREN from the core universe because the tool is for suppliers that benefit from hyperscaler spend, not hyperscalers/neoclouds spending capital.
June 30, 2026 research refresh — what changed
  • Three bottlenecks now dominate: memory/HBM, optical/network bandwidth, and power. The $700B+ capex cycle is no longer just "more GPUs."
  • Memory remains tight: AI datacenter demand is worsening the memory shortage; multiple reports point to 2027–2028 tightness and some discuss 2030. Keep memory strong at 1M–3M, but treat commodity DRAM/NAND as cyclical.
  • Networking/custom ASICs moved to center stage: Credo expects >80% FY2027 growth, Broadcom/Marvell dominate custom AI ASIC + networking discussion, Arista/Ciena benefit from AI Ethernet/optical transport, and Corning signed a multibillion-dollar Amazon fiber deal.
  • Cloud-capacity buyers are screened out: Oracle, CoreWeave, Nebius, IREN and similar names may be AI-compute demand trades, but they are not the cleanest answer to "who benefits from hyperscalers spending on datacenters?" They are capital spenders / competitors / capacity intermediaries, so the core universe now favors suppliers: networking, packaging, power, mechanical/EPC, REITs, server OEMs, EDA/IP, minerals and ETFs.
  • Advanced packaging services matter: Amkor/ASE were added because CoWoS/HBM packaging bottlenecks are broadening into outsourced packaging and test capacity; TSMC-Amkor's 10-year US packaging partnership is now part of the thesis.
  • Power contracts got more investable: Talen/NRG were added alongside Vistra/Constellation; PJM interconnect acceleration, nuclear PPAs, and gas bridge projects keep power in the 6M–1Y playbooks.

Strategy implication: the networking preset is the optical/custom-ASIC follow-through trade; servers captures OEM/integrator suppliers; buildout captures mechanical/EPC/REIT beneficiaries; designip captures the custom-ASIC design-tool layer. Hyperscalers and neoclouds are intentionally excluded from core strategies.

The supply-chain chokepoints, layer by layer

Memory & storage (spiking now)

HBM (structural) vs commodity DRAM/NAND (cyclical). Nearline HDD (Seagate/WDC, HAMR, 3-maker oligopoly) is the overlooked storage spike. HBM test = Advantest near-monopoly (Teradyne is the US proxy).

Advanced packaging

GPU output is gated by TSMC CoWoS, not wafers. ABF film = Ajinomoto near-monopoly; substrates concentrated (Ibiden/Shinko/Unimicron/AT&S). Hybrid bonding = BESI. Metrology/inspection = Onto, Camtek.

Power generation & grid

Transformers/switchgear: 2–4-yr lead times. Near-term electrons = gas gensets/aeroderivatives + fuel cells; mid-term = nuclear uprates, SMRs. The grid-iron and turbine oligopolies can't scale fast.

Power electronics

100kW+ racks force 800VDC + wide-bandgap (GaN/SiC) and vertical power delivery (Vicor, MPS, Navitas).

Optical / interconnect

Co-packaged optics & silicon photonics; transceivers (Coherent/Lumentum), Fabrinet capacity, high-speed connectors (Amphenol).

Specialty inputs

Gallium/germanium (export-controlled byproducts), high-purity quartz (Spruce Pine near-monopoly — un-investable publicly), helium (cryo/fab/fusion), high-speed CCL.

ETF proxies

There is no pure DRAM/HBM ETF. Conservative implementations use diversified proxies: SMH/SOXX/XSD for semis, GRID/PAVE/XLU for grid/infrastructure/utilities, URA/NLR for uranium/nuclear, COPX/XME for copper/metals, and FCG for natural gas.

Highest scarcity only converts to gain when it is also under-followed and investable. ASML/TSMC are Tier-S but priced; Spruce Pine quartz and private fusion are Tier-S but un-buyable. The optimizer now applies a small scarcity tilt (Tier S +0.06, A +0.025, B 0, scaled up with horizon) on top of return/risk, so higher-tier names rank a little higher — return, probability and crowding still dominate, and the tilt is off for the min-variance / risk-parity objectives.

Critical minerals — gallium, germanium, rare earths, uranium, helium
  • Gallium (GaN power, RF, optoelectronics): byproduct of alumina, China-dominant, export-controlled. Can't "open a gallium mine." Plays: 5N Plus (CA, FPLSF/VNP.TO), AXT (AXTI, China-caveat).
  • Germanium (fiber, photodetectors → optical interconnect, SiGe): zinc byproduct, China-controlled. Plays: 5N Plus, Teck (TECK), Umicore.
  • Rare earths (NdPr magnets — adjacent): Lynas (cleanest ex-China, LYSDY), MP Materials (MP, US strategic), USA Rare Earth (USAR), Energy Fuels (UUUU).
  • Uranium / HALEU: DOE flags a domestic HALEU supply gap that can delay advanced reactors. Centrus (LEU) is the closest US pure play; Cameco (CCJ) for fuel.
  • High-purity quartz: semiconductor crucibles trace to Spruce Pine, NC (Sibelco / The Quartz Corp — both private). No clean public play; systemic single-point-of-failure.
  • Helium: cryogenics, fabs, superconductors, fusion, quantum. Diluted exposure via Linde (LIN), Air Products (APD).
  • ETF proxies: no pure DRAM/HBM ETF exists; semis ETFs are only proxies. The model uses SMH, SOXX, XSD, GRID, PAVE, XLU, URA, NLR, COPX, XME, and FCG as lower-risk baskets.
Innovation triggers — gas, SMR/HALEU, geothermal, fusion, optics
  • Near-term real: gas gensets/aeroderivatives (CAT,CMI,GEV), solid-oxide fuel cells (BE), 800V/GaN (NVTS,MPWR,VICR).
  • Mid-term: SMRs (OKLO,SMR,NNE) — but HALEU (LEU) is the true chokepoint; enhanced geothermal via oilfield tech (SLB,BKR,HAL).
  • Long-dated optionality (mostly private): fusion (Helion→Microsoft PPA targeting ~2028; Commonwealth Fusion). Play the enablers: HTS/REBCO tape, cryogenics — public proxy AMSC (AMSC).
  • Architecture shift: co-packaged optics / silicon photonics (AVGO,COHR,LITE,MRVL).

Anchor datapoint (DOE/LBNL 2024): US data-center electricity rose from ~58 TWh (2014) to ~176 TWh (2023) and is projected to ~325–580 TWh by 2028 — i.e., 4.4% → 6.7–12% of US power. Power, not silicon, is the wall.

Methodology — exactly how the numbers are computed

For each asset the model holds a two-state annual return: with probability P it earns the Bull return, else a Downside. Expected annual = P·Bull + (1−P)·Downside.

  • Horizon scaling: each trigger (now / 1–3y / 3–5y / 5–10y) maps to a fraction of the annual move realized by 1M/3M/6M/1Y. "Now" front-loads; "5–10y" is mostly tail optionality.
  • Scenario/regime horizon-damps the bear/bull tilt (full at 1M, fading toward neutral by 1Y) before it multiplies the bull return and nudges P.
  • Resource runway adjusts each theme by horizon: memory and server hardware get shorter runway and larger 1Y de-rating/downside penalties if demand was already contracted; power/grid/buildout/EDA keep longer runway because interconnects, permitting, substations, design cycles and construction backlogs stretch over years.
  • Volatility at horizon = annual σ × √(years).
  • Portfolio σ = √(ΣΣ wᵢwⱼσᵢσⱼρ) with theme-aware correlation: a higher same-theme ρ (names in one layer move together) and a lower cross-theme ρ — so spreading across layers actually reduces modeled risk.
  • Optimizer goals: risk-adjusted (μ−λσ), max chance ≥ target, max return (risk-capped), minimum variance, or risk parity — each with a per-position cap and a max-positions limit. Convex mode softens the risk penalty and rewards upside tails (barbell). In Simple mode, the safe half of the slider adds an ETF-proxy tilt, so lower-risk horizons can choose baskets instead of concentrated single stocks.
  • Horizon playbooks: the “Best playbooks by horizon” table does not reuse the active portfolio. It independently scans the full universe for 1M, 3M, 6M and 1Y, then generates three profile types per horizon: high-chance, balanced, and convex (a real barbell: 60% high-chance core + 40% upside sleeve, so it keeps a positive expected return while adding tail upside). This is intentional: a short-term memory/storage trade should not be forced to share weights with a 1-year HALEU/fusion optionality basket.
  • Catalyst bias by horizon: playbooks add a transparent horizon bias on top of the math: 1M = memory/storage + immediate packaging/networking; 3M = packaging/test + optical/custom-ASIC + supplier follow-through; 6M = grid/power bridge + buildout + power electronics; 1Y = nuclear fuel, critical minerals, EDA/design IP, buildout services and frontier optionality. You can still override any output by editing rows directly.
  • Bands, downside & chance of return use a lognormal simple-return model fitted to (μ,σ), so losses are bounded at −100% (a long-only portfolio cannot lose more than its capital) and the upside is fat-tailed — more realistic than a raw Normal, which previously produced impossible sub-−100% downside figures.
  • Utility = μ − λσ (mean–variance). Convex mode adds an upside-tail bonus so "exponentially higher return" can justify risk.
  • Auto-optimize is a transparent risk-adjusted allocator: weight ∝ max(0, E[r] − λσ) (+ convex bonus), capped per position, renormalized; falls back to min-variance if nothing clears the bar. It also applies a small crowding/valuation friction to names that already look heavily repriced.

Default Bull/P/σ values are the author's judgment from the scarcity analysis, internally consistent (higher return ↔ higher σ and/or lower P). They are starting points to be edited, not forecasts.

Risks & the bear case (read before sizing anything)
  • Capex digestion / air-pocket: hyperscaler capex is the demand for the entire chain; any AI-ROI disappointment or GPU-depreciation shock pulls everything.
  • Efficiency shock (Jevons cuts both ways): cheaper compute-per-token can de-rate the most cyclical links.
  • Custom-ASIC displacement reshuffles winners; circular vendor financing can transmit stress fast.
  • Cyclical memory: commodity DRAM/NAND is a trade — the supply rush that lifts prices also ends the cycle.
  • Binary names: SMR/fusion/thin-float minerals carry political + first-of-a-kind + liquidity risk and can fall 60–90%.
  • Model risk: the two-correlation lognormal model is still simplified; real drawdowns cluster, correlations jump toward one, and crowded names gap down together.
